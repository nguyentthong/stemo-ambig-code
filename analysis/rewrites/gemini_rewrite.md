## Abstract

Video-language models (VLMs) are increasingly expected to perform complex reasoning over dynamic scenes, yet their ability to handle referential ambiguity remains a significant challenge. When a question contains a noun phrase that could refer to multiple entities—such as "the boy" in a scene with several children—an ideal system should acknowledge all possible interpretations rather than committing to one. We introduce STEMO-Ambig, a benchmark designed to evaluate how VLMs resolve such referential ambiguity across a diverse set of multi-entity video clips. Each question in our benchmark is paired with an exhaustive set of gold interpretations and their corresponding answers. Our evaluation of state-of-the-art closed and open-weight models reveals a pervasive failure mode we term "hallucinated commitment," where models treat ambiguous questions as unambiguous and provide a single, arbitrary answer. We further demonstrate that while supervised fine-tuning can teach models to adopt an enumeration format, this often results in format mimicry rather than improved grounding; models produce the expected structure but fail to correctly identify the underlying referents. By decomposing these failures into stages of perception, formatting, and substance, we show that recognizing ambiguity is insufficient for resolving it. Our findings suggest that referential ambiguity represents a distinct generation-side challenge that current training regimes and scaling efforts have yet to overcome.

## 1 Introduction

Consider a video showing two boys playing in a yard where one child slips and falls while the other continues running. When asked, "Does the boy fall down?", a grounded and helpful vision-language system should recognize that the subject is ambiguous and provide an answer for each possible referent. However, current state-of-the-art models—including the GPT-4, Gemini, and Qwen families—consistently commit to a single interpretation, answering "yes" or "no" as if the question referred to a unique individual. We call this phenomenon hallucinated commitment: the model fabricates a single intended reading of a referentially ambiguous question and discards all alternative valid interpretations. Unlike standard object hallucination, which introduces non-existent content, hallucinated commitment removes relevant content by behaving as though the linguistic input were unambiguous.

This behavior stems from a fundamental gap in how video question answering (VQA) systems are evaluated and trained. Existing benchmarks typically assume a one-to-one mapping between a question and a ground-truth answer, incentivizing models to produce concise, singular responses. Consequently, models are not evaluated on their ability to navigate the inherent uncertainty of natural language grounding in complex visual scenes. To address this, we introduce STEMO-Ambig, a benchmark specifically designed to measure referential ambiguity resolution. The dataset consists of over a thousand yes/no questions situated within multi-entity video clips, where each question is paired with an exhaustive list of gold interpretations. This allows us to move beyond simple accuracy and measure the extent to which models can enumerate all valid readings of a query.

Our systematic evaluation across multiple model families reveals that hallucinated commitment is the dominant response strategy for both proprietary and open-source VLMs. Most models fail to acknowledge ambiguity even when the number of potential referents is small, and their performance collapses entirely as the degree of ambiguity increases. This failure is not merely a matter of output length or verbosity; rather, it represents a deeper inability to link ambiguous linguistic expressions to the full set of corresponding visual entities. Even when models are prompted to "think" before answering, their internal reasoning often focuses on a single entity, ignoring the broader context required for a complete answer.

To investigate whether this behavior can be corrected through targeted training, we explore various supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning strategies. We find that while naive fine-tuning can successfully teach a model to adopt an enumeration format, this often leads to a phenomenon we describe as format mimicry. In these cases, the model learns the surface-level structure of a multi-part answer—listing several interpretations—but the substantive content of those interpretations remains incorrect or ungrounded. Even more sophisticated pipelines, such as those employing rejection sampling to preserve chain-of-thought reasoning, yield only modest improvements. This suggests that the challenge of ambiguity resolution is not merely a formatting issue but a fundamental grounding deficit that is resistant to standard fine-tuning recipes.

To better understand the mechanics of this failure, we decompose the model's task into three distinct stages: perception, format, and substance. Our analysis shows that while a model's ability to perceive ambiguity—acknowledging that multiple referents exist—is a strong predictor of whether it will attempt an enumerated format, it is a poor predictor of whether the resulting answer will be substantively correct. Models frequently recognize that a question is ambiguous yet fail to correctly identify or evaluate the specific entities involved. This bottleneck indicates that current VLMs struggle to maintain high-fidelity grounding when the task requires simultaneous tracking of multiple potential interpretations.

In summary, our work provides a rigorous framework for evaluating referential ambiguity in video-language tasks and highlights a critical limitation in current models. Our contributions are as follows:

1. We introduce STEMO-Ambig, a benchmark for referential ambiguity in video QA, featuring exhaustive gold interpretations for questions with varying degrees of ambiguity.
2. We identify and characterize "hallucinated commitment" as a pervasive failure mode across current state-of-the-art VLMs.
3. We demonstrate the "format mimicry" trap, showing that teaching models the structure of ambiguity resolution does not necessarily improve their underlying grounding capabilities.
4. We provide a stage-based decomposition of the ambiguity resolution process, revealing that the primary bottleneck lies in the transition from perceiving ambiguity to generating substantively correct multi-referent answers.
