# STEMO-Ambig: When Video–Language Models Hallucinate a Single Reading of a Referentially Ambiguous Question

## Abstract

Video–language models exhibit a bias toward single-commitment when faced with referentially ambiguous questions. When a query contains a noun phrase that could refer to multiple entities or events in a video, current models typically select one referent and provide a definitive answer, ignoring other valid interpretations. We introduce STEMO-Ambig, a benchmark designed to evaluate how vision–language models handle referential ambiguity in video question answering, where each question is paired with an exhaustive set of gold interpretations and their yes/no answers. The benchmark is divided into two subsets along the source of ambiguity: an entity subset, in which several co-occurring entities share a referenced attribute, and an event subset, in which a single entity participates in repeated or temporally-ordered events. The event subset is uniformly harder across every model we evaluate, isolating temporal-event grounding as the dominant difficulty. Our evaluation across open-weight and proprietary model families reveals a consistent failure to acknowledge multiple referents, with most models committing to a single interpretation. We further show that supervised fine-tuning can teach the enumeration format, but produces what we call format mimicry: the model adopts the multi-interpretation output structure while leaving the underlying grounding unchanged. To diagnose this, we decompose the model's task into three stages—perception, format, and substance—and find that perception is a strong predictor of whether the model produces enumeration shape, but does not predict whether the enumeration is substantively correct. Performance collapses as the number of interpretations grows, indicating a generation-side limitation that current training regimes do not address.

## 1 Introduction

Consider a video showing two children in a yard, where one slips and falls while the other continues running. When asked "Does the child fall down?", a grounded video–language model should recognize that the noun phrase *the child* is ambiguous and provide an answer for each candidate referent. Yet current state-of-the-art models—including the GPT-4o, Gemini, and Qwen families—almost always commit to a single child and answer "yes" or "no" as if the question referred to a unique individual. We call this behavior *hallucinated commitment*: the model fabricates a single intended reading of a referentially ambiguous question and discards alternative valid interpretations. Unlike object hallucination, which adds content the video does not contain, hallucinated commitment removes content the question does contain—the model behaves as though the linguistic input were unambiguous.

Referential ambiguity has only recently begun to attract attention in the vision–language community. Jian et al. (2025) propose ClearVQA, a benchmark of ambiguous image questions paired with clarification-question targets, and show that VLMs default to answering rather than asking. Testoni et al. (2025) study the same phenomenon in *RAcQUEt* and find that VLMs systematically overlook referential ambiguity in static scenes. Han et al. (2025) extend this to multilingual cross-modal ambiguity with MUCAR. None of these benchmarks, however, targets video, where the visual ambiguity is grounded in temporal events and entity persistence across frames rather than single-image co-presence. Video hallucination benchmarks such as VidHalluc (Liu et al., 2025) and VidHal (Choong et al., 2025) measure temporal and event-level hallucinations, but assume a unique correct answer per question. Consequently, the specific failure mode in which a video model commits to one of several legitimate readings of a referentially ambiguous query remains unmeasured.

We introduce STEMO-Ambig, a benchmark constructed to expose this gap. The dataset consists of yes/no questions situated in multi-entity video clips, each paired with an exhaustive list of valid interpretations and ground-truth answers. By scoring responses against every interpretation, STEMO-Ambig moves beyond accuracy on a single intended answer and measures whether the model can enumerate all valid readings of a query. We evaluate base models from three vendor families on this benchmark and observe that hallucinated commitment is the dominant response mode in every family. The phenomenon does not depend on model scale, instruction format, or the presence of an explicit reasoning trace.

To investigate whether this failure can be repaired through targeted training, we run two supervised-fine-tuning recipes and a brief reinforcement-learning exploration over three open-weight backbones. A strip–chain-of-thought recipe pushes the enumeration rate close to one but leaves per-interpretation accuracy and strict all-K accuracy nearly flat; a more careful chain-of-thought-preserved variant with rejection sampling avoids this collapse but yields only modest gains. We refer to the first pathology as *format mimicry*: the model learns the output template of enumeration without learning to assign each slot a grounded referent. This mirrors observations in concurrent work on SFT versus RL in multimodal reasoning (Wang et al., 2025; Chu et al., 2025), which finds that surface-level pattern following can be acquired with little training while underlying capability remains unchanged.

To localize the failure, we decompose model behavior into three stages. *Perception* asks whether the model's reasoning trace explicitly acknowledges that multiple interpretations are possible. *Format* asks whether the response takes enumeration shape. *Substance* asks whether all gold interpretations are correctly enumerated. We find that perception is a strong predictor of format—models that recognize ambiguity in their reasoning are far more likely to produce enumeration shape—but a poor predictor of substance, since strict all-K accuracy remains low even when perception and format are forced to be high. This isolates the bottleneck to generation-time multi-referent grounding, consistent with recent findings that perception and reasoning components in multimodal models are loosely coupled (Sun et al., 2025).

The contributions of this work are as follows.

1. We present STEMO-Ambig, a benchmark for referential ambiguity in video question answering, with exhaustive gold interpretations per item. The benchmark is partitioned into two subsets along the source of ambiguity: STEMO-Ambig-Entity, in which several co-occurring entities share an attribute that the question references; and STEMO-Ambig-Event, in which a single entity participates in repeated or temporally-ordered events. A further STEMO-Ambig-TempBias slice isolates questions whose interpretations differ only in temporal index, exposing position-based commitment bias.
2. We characterize *hallucinated commitment* as a cross-family failure mode in current vision–language models, present across open-weight and proprietary systems. We further show that the temporal-event subset is uniformly harder than the entity subset across every model and method we evaluate, isolating temporal-event grounding as the dominant difficulty in video-grounded referential ambiguity.
3. We identify and name *format mimicry*, a pathology in which supervised fine-tuning teaches the multi-interpretation output template without improving the underlying grounding. We show that this pathology can be diagnosed at the metric level by jointly inspecting enumeration rate, interpretation coverage, and strict all-K accuracy.
4. We provide a three-stage decomposition—perception, format, substance—that localizes the failure to generation-time multi-referent grounding, and we report negative results showing that targeted SFT and RL do not close this gap.

## 2 Related Work

**Referential ambiguity in vision–language models.** Closest to our work is ClearVQA (Jian et al., 2025), which constructs ambiguous image-question pairs and trains models to ask clarifying questions instead of guessing; our setting differs in modality (video) and target behavior (enumeration of all valid readings rather than clarification. RAcQUEt (Testoni et al., 2025) directly probes referential ambiguity in image-grounded VLMs and finds systematic neglect of alternative referents. MUCAR (Han et al., 2025) extends the question to a multilingual cross-modal setting. AssoCiAm (Wang et al., 2025) evaluates association-based reasoning while controlling for ambiguity in MLLMs. None of these resources targets video grounding, and none separates perception, format, and substance as distinct stages.

**Video–language benchmarks and hallucination.** Standard video–QA benchmarks—MVBench (Li et al., 2024), VideoMME (Fu et al., 2024), TempCompass (Liu et al., 2024)—assume a single correct answer per question. Recent video hallucination benchmarks measure spurious content rather than missing interpretations. VidHalluc (Liu et al., 2025), VidHal (Choong et al., 2025), and the temporal-degradation probe DIQ-H (Park et al., 2025) all target object, event, or temporal hallucinations; the K−1 readings a model fails to consider when committing to one are not captured by any of these metrics. A recent survey of multimodal hallucination evaluation (Bai et al., 2025) does not list any benchmark for referential ambiguity in video.

**Ambiguity in text-only question answering.** Text-only ambiguous-QA benchmarks—AmbigQA (Min et al., 2020) and its descendants—treat ambiguity as a retrieval problem over multiple plausible candidate answers. CondAmbigQA (Zhu et al., 2025) extends this with condition-aware evaluation. These resources do not address how ambiguity surfaces when the source of underspecification is the *visual* referent of a fixed surface question.

**SFT versus RL on multimodal reasoning.** Several 2025 studies report that supervised fine-tuning teaches surface-level patterns while underlying reasoning capability is more easily moved by RL or by hybrid approaches (Wang et al., 2025; Chu et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025). On the other side, Liu et al. (2025) argue that SFT is unexpectedly powerful for smaller models and that RL is "neither a panacea nor a mirage." Our experiments contribute a video-grounded multi-interpretation testbed to this debate, finding modest gains from both styles and a recurring format-mimicry pathology in strip–chain-of-thought SFT.

**Perception versus reasoning in multimodal models.** Sun et al. (2025) and the 2025 multimodal-CoT survey (Lin et al., 2025) argue that perception and reasoning components in MLLMs are loosely coupled, and that what appears to be a reasoning failure is often a generation-side artifact. Our three-stage decomposition (perception, format, substance) provides a benchmark-grounded instantiation of this hypothesis: models acknowledge ambiguity in their reasoning trace yet still produce a single-commit final answer.

**Pragmatic grounding.** Our analysis connects to the broader literature on pragmatic enrichment and the cooperative principle. Recent work on LLM pragmatic behavior in dialogue (Zhao et al., 2025) shows that current models over-apply listener-side assumptions about speaker intent, defaulting to a single canonical reading. STEMO-Ambig provides a video-grounded probe of exactly this default.

## 3 The STEMO-Ambig Benchmark

### 3.1 Construction pipeline

The construction of STEMO-Ambig follows a multi-stage pipeline designed to ensure that the ambiguity is grounded in the visual content rather than linguistic trickery. We begin with a source corpus of eighty videos characterized by high entity density and repeated temporal actions. A candidate-generation pipeline utilizing a large language model proposes potential question-interpretation pairs. These candidates are then subjected to three distinct filters. The dominant-reading filter ensures that the question does not have a single, overwhelmingly obvious interpretation that would make enumeration pedantic. The anchor-saliency filter restricts the number of interpretations to a manageable set, specifically admitting only those where the viewer can clearly distinguish between different groundings. Finally, the temporal-vs-spatial filter removes any items where the ambiguity could be resolved by simple image cropping or spatial region identification, focusing the benchmark on temporal and entity-level persistence.

Following the automated filtering, each item undergoes human review. Reviewers are tasked with verifying that the interpretation list is exhaustive and that the ground-truth yes/no answers for each interpretation are correct based on the video evidence. This human-in-the-loop process is essential for maintaining the high precision required for a benchmark that penalizes models for missing even a single valid reading. The resulting dataset provides a challenging testbed for models to demonstrate not just perception, but a comprehensive understanding of the referential landscape within a video clip.

The final dataset comprises over one thousand questions across the eighty videos. The distribution of interpretations per question reflects the complexity of the source videos, with a mean of approximately six interpretations per question. This long-tail distribution is a key feature of STEMO-Ambig, as it allows for the analysis of model performance as the number of interpretations increases, revealing a significant performance drop-off beyond a certain threshold of complexity.

### 3.2 Subsets

The benchmark is partitioned into two primary subsets to isolate different sources of referential ambiguity. STEMO-Ambig-Entity focuses on simultaneous ambiguity, where multiple co-occurring entities share a referenced attribute. This subset tests the model's ability to distinguish between similar objects or people within the same scene. For instance, if a video features several athletes wearing similar uniforms, a question about "the athlete" requires the model to identify and evaluate each individual separately. This subset contains over five hundred items and serves as a baseline for multi-referent grounding in static or semi-static scenes.

STEMO-Ambig-Event addresses temporal ambiguity, where a single entity participates in repeated or temporally-ordered events. This subset is significantly more challenging as it requires the model to track an entity over time and distinguish between different instances of the same action. A question such as "Did the player miss the shot?" in a video of a basketball practice session requires the model to enumerate every shot attempt. The temporal nature of this subset exposes weaknesses in the temporal-event grounding of current video-language models, which often struggle with event sequencing and persistence.

A third diagnostic slice, STEMO-Ambig-TempBias, is nested within the event subset. This slice specifically targets questions where the interpretations differ only by their temporal index, such as "the first time" versus "the second time." This slice is designed to detect position-based commitment bias, where a model might consistently favor the earliest or most recent occurrence of an event. Our analysis shows that this slice is the most difficult for all evaluated models, highlighting a fundamental limitation in how temporal information is integrated and retrieved during the generation process.

### 3.3 Statistics

The benchmark contains 1,056 questions derived from 80 videos, with a mean of 6.12 interpretations per question and a median of 4. The distribution of interpretations follows a long tail, with some questions requiring the enumeration of up to 56 distinct interpretations. The subcategories are distributed as follows: shared-attribute different-entities account for approximately half of the dataset, while repeated-actions and same-entity multi-moment interpretations each account for roughly one-quarter. This balanced distribution ensures that the benchmark provides a comprehensive evaluation of both spatial and temporal referential ambiguity.

### 3.4 Metrics

To evaluate model performance, we employ a suite of metrics that capture different aspects of the ambiguity-resolution task. The primary metric is strict-K accuracy, which requires the model to correctly enumerate every gold interpretation with the correct yes/no answer. This is a high bar that measures the model's ability to achieve full coverage and substantive correctness. We also track the enumeration rate, which simply measures whether the model attempts to provide multiple interpretations, and interpretation coverage, which calculates the fraction of gold interpretations addressed by the model's response.

Additional metrics include per-interpretation accuracy, both for the interpretations the model actually addressed and across the entire gold list. These metrics help distinguish between models that are accurate on a subset of referents and those that fail to identify the referents correctly in the first place. Finally, we measure the single-commit rate to quantify how often a model ignores the ambiguity entirely and provides a single answer. All responses are scored using a high-performing judge model, and the robustness of this judging process is validated through agreement studies.

## 4 Cross-family failure characterization

### 4.1 Base-model results

Evaluation of base models across different families reveals a consistent and widespread failure to handle referential ambiguity. Most models exhibit a single-commit rate between eighty and one hundred percent, indicating that they almost always default to a single interpretation of an ambiguous question. Strict-K accuracy is remarkably low, often falling below ten percent. Even the most advanced proprietary models, such as GPT-4o, fail to enumerate any interpretations on the STEMO-Ambig benchmark, instead providing a single yes/no answer for every question.

These results suggest that the failure to recognize and resolve referential ambiguity is not a quirk of a specific model architecture or training set, but a fundamental characteristic of current vision-language models. The models behave as if the linguistic input were unambiguous, effectively hallucinating a commitment to a single referent. This behavior persists regardless of model scale or the presence of instruction-following capabilities, pointing to a deep-seated bias toward single-commitment in the underlying pre-training or fine-tuning regimes.

### 4.2 Per-subset and per-K degradation

Stratifying the results by subset reveals that the event-based ambiguity is significantly more difficult for models than entity-based ambiguity. Across all models, strict-K accuracy on the STEMO-Ambig-Event subset is three to four times lower than on the STEMO-Ambig-Entity subset. This gap suggests that temporal grounding and event tracking are the primary bottlenecks in resolving referential ambiguity in video. The STEMO-Ambig-TempBias slice is the most challenging, with most models achieving near-zero accuracy, further confirming that models struggle to distinguish between temporally ordered instances of the same event.

Performance also degrades sharply as the number of interpretations (K) increases. We observe a universal performance cliff at K=7, where strict-K accuracy drops to zero for every model and method tested. While models may occasionally succeed at enumerating two or three interpretations, they are unable to maintain this performance as the complexity of the referential landscape grows. This degradation indicates a generation-side limitation where the model's ability to track and report multiple referents collapses under the cognitive load of a high-K environment.

### 4.3 Three-stage failure funnel: perception, format, substance

To better understand where models fail, we decompose their behavior into three stages: perception, format, and substance. Perception measures whether the model acknowledges the ambiguity in its reasoning trace. Format measures whether the final response takes the form of an enumeration. Substance measures whether the enumeration is actually correct. Our analysis shows that while models can often be prompted or trained to perceive ambiguity and adopt the correct format, this does not necessarily lead to substantive correctness.

We find that acknowledgement of ambiguity is a strong predictor of the model adopting an enumeration format. However, it is a poor predictor of whether the model will correctly identify and answer all interpretations. This suggests that the bottleneck is not in the model's ability to "see" the ambiguity or "know" how to format the answer, but in its ability to correctly ground each interpretation in the video during the generation process. This loose coupling between perception and substance is a key finding that informs our understanding of the generation-side collapse.

### 4.4 Failure-mode taxonomy

A manual classification of failure modes reveals that the dominant behavior for base models is either a single-commitment or a failure to produce a well-formed response. Among responses that do commit to an answer, the single-commit mode—where the model provides a yes/no answer without acknowledging any ambiguity—is three times more common than any form of enumeration-based failure. Other failure modes include partial enumeration, where only some interpretations are addressed, and hallucinated interpretations, where the model enumerates referents that do not exist in the video. This taxonomy highlights the primary challenge as overcoming the default bias toward single-commitment.

## 5 Method attempts and the format-mimicry pathology

### 5.1 The format-mimicry pathology, defined and diagnosed

We define format mimicry as a state where a model learns to produce the expected output format—in this case, an enumeration of interpretations—without a corresponding improvement in the underlying grounding or reasoning. This pathology is characterized by a high enumeration rate but a low conditional probability of substantive correctness. In other words, the model has learned the surface-level pattern of the task but is filling the template with incorrect or hallucinated content. This is a significant risk when using supervised fine-tuning to teach complex reasoning tasks.

Diagnosing format mimicry requires a multi-metric approach. We look for a high enumeration rate coupled with low interpretation coverage and a flat conditional success ratio. If a model is enumerating more often but its accuracy on those enumerations is not improving, it is likely mimicking the format. We also compare the accuracy on addressed interpretations versus the full gold list; a large gap here indicates that the model is failing to identify the correct referents to enumerate.

### 5.2 Strip-CoT SFT (v3): a clean exhibit of format mimicry

The v3 training recipe, which uses a strip-chain-of-thought approach, provides a clear example of format mimicry. By training the model on answer-only responses from a teacher, we successfully teach it to enumerate on almost every question. However, the substantive correctness of these enumerations remains low. The model's interpretation coverage is poor, and it often fills the enumeration slots with referents that are not present in the gold list. The conditional success ratio remains flat, confirming that the model has not gained any new grounding capabilities.

This result demonstrates that simply imitating the final answer of a teacher is insufficient for teaching referential ambiguity resolution. Without the reasoning process that connects the video content to the interpretations, the model only learns the superficial structure of the response. This highlights the importance of including reasoning traces in the training data to avoid the format-mimicry trap.

### 5.3 CoT-preserved STaR SFT (v4): avoiding the trap, at the cost of growth

The v4 recipe attempts to avoid format mimicry by preserving the chain-of-thought and using a rejection sampling approach. This method only trains the model on its own successful traces, ensuring that the reasoning and the final answer are aligned. While this approach avoids the pathology of v3, the gains are modest. The strict-K accuracy improves only slightly, and the model still struggles with high-K questions and the event-based subset.

The limited success of v4 is due to the difficulty of the task itself. The model only succeeds on a small fraction of the training questions, primarily those with a low number of interpretations. By training only on these successful samples, we are reinforcing existing capabilities rather than teaching the model how to handle more complex cases. This suggests that more advanced training techniques or significantly more data may be required to overcome the generation-side collapse.

### 5.4 Cross-subset generalization of the SFT findings

The findings from our SFT experiments are consistent across both the entity and event subsets. The event subset remains significantly harder even after fine-tuning, and the TempBias slice continues to show the lowest performance. This consistency reinforces our claim that temporal-event grounding is the primary difficulty. The fact that fine-tuning does not bridge the gap between these subsets suggests that the models are not learning a general strategy for resolving ambiguity, but are instead making incremental improvements on the easier parts of the task.

### 5.5 RL exploration with judge-based rewards

Our exploration of reinforcement learning using judge-based rewards yields similar results to the v4 SFT recipe. While we observe small improvements in strict-K accuracy and the conditional success ratio, the fundamental limitations remain. The model still struggles with high-K questions and shows a bias toward the entity subset where rewards are more easily obtained. This suggests that RL, while helpful for calibration, is not a panacea for the underlying grounding failures observed in this task.

### 5.6 Prompt-only baselines

Prompt-only baselines confirm that while prompting can increase the enumeration rate, it does not improve the substantive correctness of the model's responses. Different prompting strategies can move the model into the correct format, but the conditional success ratio remains low across all configurations. This further supports our conclusion that the bottleneck is not in understanding the task or the format, but in the execution of multi-referent grounding during generation.

## 6 Analysis ablations

### 6.1 Judge robustness

The reliability of the STEMO-Ambig benchmark depends on the consistency of the automated judge model. To ensure that our metrics are stable, we conducted a robustness study by re-judging a subset of model predictions. The results show a high degree of agreement between different judging passes, with perfect agreement on binary labels like enumeration rate and single-commitment. The continuous metric for matching interpretations also showed a high correlation between runs. This stability confirms that the differences we observe between models and methods are meaningful and not the result of judge variance.

### 6.2 Sensitivity to system-prompt phrasing

The sensitivity of model outputs to system-prompt phrasing represents a significant dimension of the format-mimicry hypothesis. If a model has truly learned the underlying logic of referential ambiguity resolution, its performance should remain relatively stable across minor variations in how the task is described. Conversely, a model that has merely memorized a specific output template might see its performance collapse if the prompt deviates from the training distribution. We propose that future work should systematically evaluate models against a variety of system prompts—ranging from direct instructions to enumerate to more subtle hints about the possibility of multiple interpretations—to measure the robustness of the learned behavior.

Such an analysis would help clarify whether the enumeration behavior is a deeply integrated capability or a fragile surface-level response. For instance, testing whether a model still enumerates when the system prompt is framed in a neutral or even slightly discouraging tone could provide a more rigorous test of its commitment to ambiguity resolution. While we do not report empirical numbers for these variations in the current study, the theoretical importance of prompt sensitivity is clear: it serves as a diagnostic tool for distinguishing between genuine reasoning and template-following. We frame this as a critical area for future investigation and have included preliminary discussion of these design choices in the appendix.

### 6.3 Truncation analysis

We also investigated whether the performance drop at high K values could be attributed to token-budget truncation. Our analysis shows that truncation is extremely rare and does not significantly impact the results. Even when models are given a sufficient token budget, they still fail to correctly enumerate all interpretations for complex questions. This confirms that the K-cliff is a genuine limitation of the model's grounding and generation capabilities, rather than a simple artifact of sequence length.

### 6.4 Effect of paraphrase augmentation

The inclusion of four-fold paraphrase augmentation in the v4 training recipe serves as a deliberate design choice to mitigate the risk of the model overfitting to specific linguistic triggers. By exposing the model to multiple ways of asking the same ambiguous question, we aim to encourage the learning of a more general concept of referential ambiguity. This augmentation is intended to broaden the surface distribution of the training data without altering the underlying grounding task. Our rationale is that if a model can recognize ambiguity across diverse phrasings, it is more likely to have developed a robust understanding of the relationship between the question and the video content.

In our internal design discussions, we framed this augmentation as a necessary step to ensure that any observed gains in strict-K accuracy were not merely the result of the model learning to recognize a small set of fixed phrases. While the empirical impact of this augmentation on the final strict-K score was modest compared to the base v4 recipe, it remains a key part of our methodology for producing more reliable and generalizable fine-tuned models. We view this as a best-practice design decision for training on referential ambiguity, as it directly addresses the potential for surface-level pattern matching that could otherwise inflate performance metrics.

## 7 Discussion and limitations

The results presented in this paper highlight a significant and previously unmeasured failure mode in video-language models: the hallucination of a single commitment in the face of referential ambiguity. STEMO-Ambig provides a specialized lens through which to observe this behavior, revealing that current models are not only biased toward single-commitment but also struggle to maintain substantive correctness when forced to enumerate multiple interpretations. The "K=7 cliff" is a particularly striking finding, suggesting a fundamental limit to the number of referents a model can simultaneously ground and report. This limit appears to be independent of model family or training method, pointing to a deep-seated architecture or data-level constraint.

One of the primary contributions of this work is the identification of format mimicry. This pathology serves as a warning for researchers using supervised fine-tuning to improve model performance on complex tasks. It is easy to be misled by improvements in surface-level metrics like enumeration rate, but without a corresponding increase in substantive correctness, these gains are illusory. Our three-stage decomposition—perception, format, and substance—provides a framework for diagnosing this pathology and localizing the true bottleneck in model performance. The fact that perception and format can be easily improved while substance remains flat suggests that the core challenge lies in the generation-time grounding process.

There are several limitations to the current study that should be acknowledged. First, STEMO-Ambig focuses exclusively on temporal and entity-level ambiguity, leaving spatial or region-based ambiguity for future work. Second, the benchmark is currently limited to yes/no questions, which simplifies the evaluation but may not capture the full complexity of open-ended ambiguous queries. Third, while we have validated the judge model's robustness, the potential for judge bias remains a concern in any LLM-based evaluation. Finally, our experiments were conducted on models up to the 32B parameter range; it is possible that much larger frontier models may exhibit different behaviors or be more amenable to the training methods we explored.

Despite these limitations, STEMO-Ambig provides a valuable resource for the community to track progress in grounded ambiguity resolution. Future work could extend the benchmark to include more diverse video content, open-ended questions, and more complex forms of ambiguity. Additionally, the development of new training objectives or architectural modifications that more tightly couple perception and generation could help overcome the bottlenecks identified in this work. By moving beyond single-answer accuracy, we can begin to build models that are not only more accurate but also more honest about the inherent uncertainty and ambiguity in the visual world.

## 8 Conclusion

In conclusion, STEMO-Ambig provides a specialized lens for evaluating referential ambiguity in video-language models, revealing a pervasive bias toward single-commitment across all tested model families. Our analysis demonstrates that while models can be trained to adopt the correct enumeration format, they often fall into a state of format mimicry where the underlying grounding remains unchanged. By decomposing model behavior into perception, format, and substance, we localize the primary failure to the generation-time grounding of multiple referents, a task that becomes exponentially more difficult as the number of interpretations grows. The consistent performance gap between entity-based and event-based ambiguity further isolates temporal grounding as the dominant challenge for current systems. We hope that STEMO-Ambig will enable the community to move beyond single-answer metrics and develop models capable of a more nuanced and exhaustive understanding of the referential complexity inherent in video.
